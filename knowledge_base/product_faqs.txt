A1. UNDERSTANDING LAPTOP SPECIFICATIONS

Q: What do the processor numbers actually mean — and does it matter?
A: The processor (CPU) is the brain of the laptop. The two main manufacturers are Intel
and AMD. Intel uses a naming scheme like Core i5-1335U or Core Ultra 7 155H. AMD uses
Ryzen 5 7530U or Ryzen 9 7945HX. As a general guide:
Core i3 / Ryzen 3: suitable for basic tasks — web browsing, email, document editing.
Core i5 / Ryzen 5: the sweet spot for most people — handles everyday tasks, light photo
editing, streaming, and video calls comfortably.
Core i7 / Ryzen 7: for more demanding use — video editing, running multiple applications,
intensive spreadsheets, light gaming.
Core i9 / Ryzen 9: professional-level performance — 3D rendering, heavy video production,
data science workloads.
The letter at the end matters too: U means ultra-low power (good battery life, suitable
for thin and light laptops); H or HX means high performance (faster but runs hotter and
uses more battery — common in gaming laptops).
NOTE FOR AGENTS: a customer asking "is this laptop fast enough for my job?" needs a
conversation about what software they run, not just a spec comparison. Ask specifically:
Do you use any specialist software? Do you edit photos or video? Do you game?

Q: How much RAM do I need?
A: RAM (memory) determines how many things your laptop can do at once before slowing down.
8GB RAM: minimum for Windows 11; acceptable for light use but can feel sluggish with many
browser tabs open. We do not recommend 8GB for new purchases in 2026.
16GB RAM: the recommended standard. Handles everyday multitasking, light creative work,
and most business applications comfortably.
32GB RAM: for creative professionals, software developers, running virtual machines,
or heavy multitasking.
64GB RAM: specialist use only — data science, professional video production, 3D modelling.
IMPORTANT: many modern laptops, particularly those under £600, have RAM soldered directly
to the motherboard and cannot be upgraded later. If a customer is choosing between 8GB
(not upgradeable) and 16GB (not upgradeable), always recommend 16GB — the difference in
cost now is far less than the cost of replacing the laptop in two years.

Q: SSD vs HDD — which is better?
A: All new laptops sold by Voltex since 2024 include SSD (solid state drive) storage.
HDDs (hard disk drives) are spinning mechanical drives that are slower, heavier, and more
vulnerable to damage from drops. SSDs are faster, quieter, lighter, and more durable.
If a customer is asking about this, they may be comparing a new Voltex laptop to a
second-hand or very old device. There is no scenario in our current range where an HDD
is preferable in a laptop.
Storage sizes:
256GB SSD: minimum — adequate for the operating system and basic files, but fills quickly
if the customer stores photos, videos, or large applications. Not recommended for most users.
512GB SSD: the practical minimum for most people — comfortable for everyday use.
1TB SSD: recommended for anyone who stores significant media, works with large files,
or doesn't want to think about storage management.

Q: What is the difference between integrated graphics and a dedicated GPU?
A: Integrated graphics means the processor handles visuals using shared system memory.
This is fine for everyday tasks, streaming video, and light creative work.
A dedicated GPU (graphics processing unit) is a separate chip with its own memory,
designed for visually intensive tasks. You need a dedicated GPU for:
- Gaming (most games require at least an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3050 or equivalent to run well)
- Video editing with hardware acceleration (Premiere Pro, DaVinci Resolve)
- 3D modelling (Blender, AutoCAD, SolidWorks)
- Machine learning and AI development work
- Running multiple external monitors at high resolution simultaneously
Common dedicated GPU brands in our range: NVIDIA GeForce RTX series (consumer and gaming),
NVIDIA RTX for professional workstations, AMD Radeon RX series.
AGENT NOTE: if a customer says they want to "do a bit of gaming" — ask what games. Minecraft
and browser games run on integrated graphics. Call of Duty, Cyberpunk, and FIFA require
a dedicated GPU. This is the single most common reason customers return laptops.

Q: What does the display type mean — IPS, OLED, AMOLED, TN?
A: Display technology affects colour accuracy, viewing angles, and brightness.
TN (Twisted Nematic): fast refresh rates, found in budget gaming laptops. Poor viewing
angles and colour accuracy. Less common in new laptops in our 2026 range.
IPS (In-Plane Switching): good colour accuracy, wide viewing angles. The standard for
most laptops in our range. Suitable for most users.
OLED / AMOLED: exceptional colour accuracy, perfect blacks, high contrast. Preferred by
creative professionals and those who watch a lot of video content. More expensive and
higher battery consumption than IPS. Note: OLED screens on laptops are susceptible to
burn-in over time if static elements (taskbar, desktop icons) are left on screen for
extended periods. This is a known characteristic, not a defect.
Refresh rate: standard is 60Hz. Gaming laptops offer 120Hz, 144Hz, or 165Hz — higher
refresh rates make motion smoother during gaming. For non-gaming use, 60Hz is sufficient.
Resolution: Full HD (1920x1080) is standard. 2K (2560x1440) and 4K (3840x2160) offer
sharper images but reduce battery life and require more GPU power to run.

A2. CHOOSING THE RIGHT LAPTOP FOR YOUR NEEDS

Q: I just need something for everyday use — web, email, video calls. What should I get?
A: For this use case: Core i5 or Ryzen 5 processor, 16GB RAM, 512GB SSD, IPS display.
Budget of £500–£700 covers this specification comfortably in our current range.
Brands to consider: Lenovo IdeaPad and ThinkPad E series, HP Pavilion and Envy series,
ASUS VivoBook series. All offer good everyday performance at this price point.
Battery life matters for everyday use: look for 10+ hours quoted battery life. Real-world
battery life is typically 60–70% of quoted figures under mixed use.

Q: I am a student — what laptop do I need?
A: Depends heavily on the subject. Ask the customer:
Arts, humanities, business, law: everyday use spec is sufficient (see above).
Engineering, architecture, design (CAD, 3D modelling): needs a dedicated GPU and 16GB+ RAM.
Computer science / software development: 16GB RAM recommended; developer tools are memory-hungry.
Media production (video, music): dedicated GPU and 1TB+ storage. Consider a display with
good colour accuracy (IPS minimum, OLED preferred).
For students who need Microsoft Office: check whether their university provides free access
through Microsoft 365 Education before recommending a laptop with Office included.

Q: I want to use my laptop for gaming. What do I need?
A: The key specs for gaming are:
Dedicated GPU: minimum NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3050 for modern titles at 1080p. RTX 4060 or
above for high settings or 1440p gaming.
Processor: Core i7 / Ryzen 7 minimum for gaming. The CPU affects how fast the game
simulates the world; the GPU renders what you see.
RAM: 16GB minimum; 32GB recommended for future-proofing.
Display: 144Hz refresh rate minimum for a smooth gaming experience. 165Hz preferred.
Storage: 1TB SSD minimum — modern games are 50–100GB each.
Cooling: gaming laptops run hot. Ask if the customer will mainly use it on a desk (better
airflow) or on their lap (restricts vents). For lap use, consider a laptop cooling pad.
Battery: gaming laptops have poor battery life under gaming load — typically 1 to 2 hours.
They are best used plugged in. This is expected behaviour, not a fault.

Q: Is a Chromebook suitable for me?
A: Chromebooks run ChromeOS and are designed primarily for cloud-based work. They are
an excellent choice if the customer:
- Primarily uses web-based tools (Google Docs, Gmail, web browsing, streaming)
- Wants a lightweight, low-cost device primarily for school (KS3/GCSE level)
- Wants very long battery life (Chromebooks typically achieve 12–15 hours real-world)
Chromebooks are NOT suitable if the customer needs to:
- Run Windows software (Microsoft Office desktop app, Adobe suite, specialist software)
- Play PC games
- Use professional creative or engineering tools
NOTE: Microsoft Office is available as a web app on Chromebooks. This is not the same
as the full desktop application — functionality is reduced. If a customer's workplace
or college uses specific Windows software, a Chromebook will not meet their needs.
VoltCare: Essential and Plus only on Chromebooks. Complete is not available.

Q: Can I use my laptop with an external monitor or TV?
A: Most modern laptops support external displays via HDMI, USB-C, or Thunderbolt.
Check the specific laptop's port configuration:
HDMI out: connects directly to any TV or monitor with an HDMI input.
USB-C / Thunderbolt: requires a compatible monitor or an adapter/dock.
DisplayPort: found on some business laptops and gaming laptops; very high bandwidth.
Maximum display support varies: most laptops support 1 or 2 external monitors.
Some (particularly those with Thunderbolt 4) support up to 4 displays via a dock.
NOTE: connecting a laptop to a TV does not automatically make it a "smart TV." The laptop
drives the content; the TV is a display only. The TV's own smart features are unrelated.

A3. LAPTOPS — COMMON ISSUES AND TROUBLESHOOTING GUIDANCE

Q: My laptop is running slowly — what could cause this?
A: Common causes and what they mean for VoltCare/repair eligibility:
Too many startup programmes (not a fault — advise customer to check Task Manager > Startup)
Storage nearly full: performance drops significantly when SSD is over 90% full. Not a fault.
Background updates running: Windows Update or antivirus scans slow performance temporarily.
RAM insufficient for workload: if the customer has 8GB and runs many tabs/apps, the laptop
will slow down. Not a fault — this is a specification limitation.
Overheating causing throttling: if the laptop fan runs constantly and performance degrades,
this may indicate a cooling fault or dust accumulation. Covered under VoltCare as a
mechanical fault if confirmed by repair centre.
Hardware failure (failing SSD, degrading RAM): covered under VoltCare and statutory rights.

Q: My laptop battery drains very quickly
A: Battery degradation over time is expected. Covered under VoltCare if:
The battery capacity has dropped significantly within the first 12 months (premature failure).
The battery fails completely (will not charge or hold any charge).
NOT covered if the battery has naturally degraded after heavy use over 2+ years — this is
normal wear. A battery at 70% of original capacity after 3 years of daily use is not a fault.
For context: most laptop batteries are rated for 300–500 full charge cycles before noticeable
degradation. Charging daily = approximately 1 cycle per day = 300-500 days before degradation
becomes significant. Heavy users who charge multiple times a day will see faster degradation.
NOTE: features like Adaptive Battery (Windows 11) and battery health management can extend
battery lifespan significantly if enabled. Agents can advise customers to check these settings
before concluding the battery is faulty.

B1. UNDERSTANDING TV SPECIFICATIONS

Q: What is the difference between OLED, QLED, and LED TVs?
A: These refer to the display technology used to produce the image.
LED (standard): uses a backlight behind an LCD panel. The most common and affordable
technology. Good brightness, good for well-lit rooms. Black levels are less deep than
OLED because the backlight cannot be fully turned off.
QLED (Quantum LED — Samsung's branding, also used by TCL and Hisense): an enhanced LED
TV using quantum dot technology to improve colour volume and brightness. Excellent for
bright rooms and HDR content. Does not match OLED for black levels but often brighter.
OLED: each pixel produces its own light and can switch off completely, producing perfect
blacks and infinite contrast ratio. Exceptional for dark room viewing, cinematic content,
and gaming (near-zero response time). More expensive than LED/QLED. Susceptible to
burn-in (see A4 below). Brands: LG (WOLED), Sony (using LG panels), Philips.
OLED evo / MLA OLED (LG): enhanced OLED with higher brightness — addresses the
traditional OLED weakness in bright rooms.
QD-OLED (Samsung, Sony): combines OLED pixel self-emission with quantum dot colour
layer. Considered the premium choice in 2025/26 — very high brightness, perfect blacks,
exceptional colour. Sony Bravia 9 and Samsung S95D use this technology.
MINI-LED: an LED TV with thousands of tiny LEDs for the backlight rather than a few
large ones, allowing much finer local dimming. Achieves near-OLED black levels while
maintaining LED brightness advantages. Found in Samsung Neo QLED, LG QNED, TCL QM series.

Q: What screen size should I buy?
A: Screen size should be matched to viewing distance and room size. As a guide:
43–50 inch: suitable for bedrooms or small living rooms, viewing distance 1.5–2.5m
55 inch: the most popular living room size — good for viewing distances of 2–3m
65 inch: ideal for viewing distances of 2.5–3.5m; increasingly the mainstream choice
75 inch: large living rooms, viewing distance 3–4m minimum for comfortable viewing
85 inch and above: home cinema rooms or very large open-plan spaces
NOTE: many customers underestimate how large a TV looks once mounted on a wall in their
home vs in a large showroom. If a customer is unsure between two sizes, for living room
use, the larger size is rarely regretted. For bedrooms, the smaller size is usually better.

Q: What is 4K and do I need it?
A: 4K (also called Ultra HD or UHD) refers to a resolution of approximately 3840 x 2160
pixels — four times the detail of Full HD (1080p). Virtually all TVs 43 inches and above
sold by Voltex in 2026 are 4K.
Do you need 4K? For a TV 43 inches and above, yes — the extra detail is visible at normal
viewing distances and all major streaming services (Netflix, Disney+, Amazon Prime Video)
stream in 4K by default on compatible TVs.
For a bedroom TV under 40 inches, Full HD may be sufficient depending on viewing distance.
8K TVs are available in our range (75 inches and above from Samsung and LG). 8K content
is extremely limited in 2026 — there is no mainstream 8K streaming or broadcast. 8K TVs
upscale 4K content to 8K, which offers marginal improvement. We do not actively recommend
8K for typical customers.

Q: What is HDR and why does it matter?
A: HDR (High Dynamic Range) allows the TV to display a wider range of brightness —
from very dark shadows to very bright highlights — within the same image.
HDR10: the baseline HDR standard. Supported by virtually all 4K TVs and all streaming
services. You need this as a minimum.
HDR10+: Samsung's dynamic HDR standard — adjusts HDR settings scene by scene rather than
for the whole film. Supported by Amazon Prime Video and Samsung TVs. Not supported by LG OLED.
Dolby Vision: the premium HDR format, developed by Dolby. Adjusts HDR at a frame-by-frame
level. Supported by Netflix, Disney+, Apple TV. Found on LG, Sony, Philips, and TCL TVs.
Not supported on Samsung TVs (Samsung uses HDR10+ instead).
HLG (Hybrid Log Gamma): used for live broadcast HDR content — BBC and Channel 4 broadcast
in HLG. All 4K TVs in our range support HLG.
AGENT NOTE: a customer asking "does this TV support HDR?" should be asked which streaming
services they use. Netflix and Disney+ users want Dolby Vision (not on Samsung). Amazon
Prime Video users want HDR10+ (Samsung and others). If they use both, they may want a TV
that supports both formats — check the specific model's spec sheet.

Q: What is the difference between refresh rate and motion handling?
A: Refresh rate is how many times per second the screen updates the image, measured in Hz.
50Hz / 60Hz: standard. Fine for watching films and TV shows.
100Hz / 120Hz: smoother motion. Visually better for sport and for gaming.
Manufacturers advertise "effective" refresh rates (Motionflow, TruMotion, PurMotion)
that use processing to simulate higher frame rates. A 60Hz TV with motion processing
marketed as "120Hz effective" is NOT the same as a native 120Hz panel.
AGENT NOTE: for sports viewers and gamers, always check the native panel refresh rate,
not the marketed processing rate. Native 120Hz panels are found in mid-range and above
TVs from Samsung, LG, and Sony. Budget and entry-level TVs typically have 60Hz native panels.
For gaming specifically: native 120Hz with HDMI 2.1 port is required to benefit from
120fps gaming on PlayStation 5 and Xbox Series X. Not all HDMI ports on a TV are HDMI 2.1
— check which ports are HDMI 2.1 if this matters to the customer.

Q: What smart TV platforms are available and what are the differences?
A: The smart platform determines how you navigate the TV, which apps are available,
and how well it integrates with other devices.
Samsung Tizen: Samsung's own platform. Excellent app selection, strong integration with
Samsung smartphones and Galaxy ecosystem. Samsung Gaming Hub for cloud gaming.
LG webOS: considered by many reviewers as the most user-friendly smart platform.
Strong app support, excellent ThinQ app for smartphone integration. Magic Remote pointer
navigation is a popular feature.
Google TV (Sony Bravia, TCL, Hisense): runs Google's TV platform. Best integration with
Android phones and Google services. Very large app catalogue via Google Play Store.
Voice control via Google Assistant built in.
Amazon Fire TV (available on some Hisense and Toshiba models in our range):
best integration with Amazon Prime Video and Alexa ecosystem.
NOTE: all major streaming apps (Netflix, Disney+, Amazon Prime Video, Apple TV+, BBC iPlayer,
ITVX, Channel 4, YouTube) are available on all four platforms. The differences are in
navigation preference and ecosystem integration, not fundamental capability.
Customers asking "can I get [specific app]?" — check the TV's product page for app
availability. Niche apps (specific sports packages, regional broadcasters) may vary by platform.

B2. BUYING GUIDE — TELEVISIONS

Q: I want a TV mainly for watching sport. What should I look for?
A: Key specs for sport viewing:
Native 120Hz refresh rate: essential for smooth fast motion. Do not accept 60Hz with
motion processing as a substitute.
High brightness: sport is often watched during the day in well-lit rooms. QLED, Mini-LED,
or OLED evo are better choices than standard OLED for bright room viewing.
Low input lag: relevant if the customer watches sport with the sound from a separate audio
system — a TV with high input lag causes audio sync issues.
For customers who also want to use the TV for gaming: HDMI 2.1 port is required for 120fps.

Q: I want to use my TV for gaming with a PlayStation 5 or Xbox Series X. What do I need?
A: The TV must have at least one HDMI 2.1 port to access 4K 120fps gaming.
All current-gen consoles also benefit from VRR (Variable Refresh Rate) — reduces screen
tearing during gameplay. This requires both the console and the TV to support VRR.
Most HDMI 2.1 TVs in our 2026 range support VRR.
ALLM (Auto Low Latency Mode): the TV automatically switches to Game Mode when a console
is detected, reducing input lag without the user needing to manually change settings.
Recommended brands for PS5/Xbox gaming: LG OLED (exceptional gaming features, near-zero
input lag), Samsung QN90D/QN95D (very high brightness game mode), Sony Bravia 9 (Sony's
own console ecosystem integration).

Q: What is OLED burn-in and should I be worried?
A: OLED burn-in occurs when a static image is displayed for very long periods, causing
that image to be permanently retained in the screen. This is an inherent characteristic
of OLED technology and is NOT covered under VoltCare or any manufacturer warranty.
In practice, burn-in is not a concern for most viewers who watch varied content.
It is more likely to occur if:
- The TV is used as a PC monitor with a static taskbar or desktop icons
- News channels with persistent tickers or logos are watched for many hours daily
- The same game HUD or static UI element is displayed for hundreds of hours
All LG and Sony OLED TVs include pixel refresher tools that reduce burn-in risk when
used regularly. We recommend running the pixel refresher overnight monthly.
For customers who primarily watch varied content (films, drama, sport): burn-in risk is low.
For customers who want to use the TV as a PC monitor or play a single game for hundreds
of hours: consider a QLED or Mini-LED TV instead of OLED.

For Sky Glass: Sky Glass is not a standard TV — it is a Sky product sold by Sky directly.
Voltex does not sell Sky Glass. Customers asking about Sky Glass should be directed to sky.com.
NOTE: some properties (flats, listed buildings) have restrictions on external aerials and
dishes. This is a property management or planning issue, not something Voltex can resolve.

C1. WASHING MACHINES

Q: What does the drum size mean and what size do I need?
A: Drum size is measured in kilograms — the maximum dry weight of laundry per wash.
6kg: suitable for 1–2 people. A full 6kg load is roughly 3 pairs of jeans, 6 shirts,
and some underwear.
8kg: suitable for 2–3 people. The most popular size in our range.
10kg: suitable for 3–4 people or families who prefer to do fewer, larger washes.
12kg and above: large families, or customers who regularly wash bulky items like duvets
and curtains without needing a laundrette.
NOTE: drum size affects the machine's physical dimensions only slightly — an 8kg and 10kg
machine from the same brand are typically the same width and height, just different depth.
Customers in tight spaces should always check the product dimensions, not just the drum size.

Q: What is an inverter motor and why does it matter?
A: An inverter motor adjusts its speed electronically rather than switching on and off at
a fixed speed. Benefits:
- Quieter operation — significant if the machine is in a kitchen or open-plan space
- More energy efficient — the motor uses only the power needed for the current load
- Longer lifespan — fewer mechanical stresses than a conventional motor
- Most manufacturers offering inverter motors back them with a 10-year motor warranty
  (Bosch, Samsung, LG, Beko all offer this on selected models in our range)
Brands known for inverter motor reliability in our 2026 range: Bosch Series 6 and above,
Samsung Ecobubble, LG F series, Beko ProSmart.

Q: What do the energy ratings mean (A, B, C, D)?
A: EU and UK energy labels rate appliances from A (most efficient) to G (least efficient).
For washing machines as of 2026:
A-rated machines: the most efficient available. Significantly lower energy bills over the
appliance's lifetime but carry a premium purchase price.
B and C-rated machines: good efficiency — the majority of our mid-range and budget models.
D-rated and below: less efficient. Fine for customers on a tight budget but higher running costs.
Energy calculation guidance: an A-rated 8kg washing machine uses approximately 50–60 kWh
per 100 cycles. At 24p per kWh (approximate 2026 UK rate), this is around £13–£14 per
100 washes. A D-rated machine may use 80–90 kWh per 100 cycles (£19–£22 per 100 washes).
Over 1,000 washes, the difference is approximately £60–£80 in running costs — useful context
when a customer questions whether an A-rated machine is worth the higher price.

Q: Will a new washing machine fit in my kitchen?
A: Standard washing machine dimensions: 60cm wide, 60cm deep, 85cm tall. This is universal
across brands and fits standard kitchen spaces designed for washing machines.
Slim-line washing machines (45cm wide): available for narrow spaces. Drum sizes are
smaller (typically 6–7kg maximum) and the range is more limited.
Integrated washing machines: designed to fit behind a kitchen door. Require a specific
integrated-compatible cabinet opening. Not all washing machines are integrated-compatible —
check the product listing for "integrated" or "freestanding" classification. An integrated
machine cannot simply be placed freestanding — the door mounting mechanism is different.
NOTE: the customer must measure the width of their access route (doorways, corridors) as
well as the installation space. A washing machine that fits the space but cannot get through
a narrow doorway is a real and common problem with large capacity machines.

Q: What spin speed should I choose?
A: Spin speed is measured in RPM (revolutions per minute) and determines how dry clothes
are at the end of the cycle. Higher spin speed = drier clothes = less drying time needed.
1000 RPM: basic — clothes will be noticeably damp. Higher drying time needed.
1200 RPM: the practical minimum we recommend. Standard for most households.
1400 RPM: the most common and recommended. Clothes are significantly drier.
1600 RPM: diminishing returns — marginal improvement over 1400 RPM for most fabrics,
but the machine may be noisier on the spin cycle.
NOTE: high spin speeds can damage delicate fabrics. All modern washing machines have
dedicated delicate, wool, and silk programmes that automatically reduce spin speed —
customers do not need to worry about selecting spin speed manually for these items.

C2. FRIDGE-FREEZERS

Q: What is the difference between a frost-free and a manual defrost fridge-freezer?
A: Frost-free (also called No Frost or Total No Frost): the appliance automatically
prevents ice build-up in the freezer compartment. No manual defrosting required.
The fan circulates cold air throughout the freezer, maintaining consistent temperature.
Slight running cost increase vs manual defrost, but the convenience is considerable.
Recommended for most households. All fridge-freezers in our Complete tier are frost-free.
Manual defrost: ice builds up in the freezer compartment over time and must be manually
defrosted periodically (typically every 6–12 months). Lower purchase price. Suitable for
customers who prefer simplicity or are on a tight budget.
NOTE: "Frost-free fridge" refers to the fridge compartment only — some models are
frost-free in the fridge but require manual defrosting in the freezer. Check product
spec for "Total No Frost" (both compartments) vs "No Frost Fridge" (fridge only).

Q: What size fridge-freezer do I need?
A: Total capacity is measured in litres, divided between fridge and freezer sections.
Single person or couple: 200–250 litres total (roughly 60/40 fridge/freezer split)
Family of 3–4: 300–400 litres total
Large family or bulk shoppers: 400–500 litres
American-style (side-by-side) fridge-freezers: typically 500–700 litres total.
These are significantly wider (90–100cm) than standard models. Customers must measure
the space carefully — American-style appliances often cannot fit in a standard kitchen
alcove designed for a standard 60cm fridge-freezer.
NOTE: fridge-freezer dimensions are given as Height x Width x Depth. Height is the most
variable dimension — some models are 180cm, some are 200cm. Customers with low ceilings
or overhead cupboards must check height before purchasing.

Q: My fridge is making a strange noise — is it broken?
A: Some noises from fridge-freezers are entirely normal. Help customers distinguish:
Normal noises:
- Gurgling or bubbling: refrigerant flowing through the cooling system — normal.
- Clicking when compressor starts or stops — normal.
- Humming from the compressor running — normal.
- Frost-free models: fan noise is normal; louder airflow is expected compared to manual defrost.
Noises that may indicate a fault:
- Loud rattling: may indicate a loose component or the appliance vibrating against a wall
  or cabinet. First ask: is the appliance level? Is it touching a wall or adjacent cabinet?
  Check that the transit bolts (on washing machines, also relevant check for fridges on delivery)
  have been removed. If levelling does not resolve it, log as a potential fault.
- Loud continuous grinding or knocking: may indicate a fan blade obstructed by ice build-up
  (on manual defrost models) or a failing compressor. This is a covered fault.
- High-pitched buzzing or squealing: potential fan bearing fault. Log as a fault.

C3. DISHWASHERS

Q: What size dishwasher do I need — full size or slimline?
A: Full size (60cm wide): fits 13–15 place settings. Standard for most kitchens.
Slimline (45cm wide): fits 9–10 place settings. For smaller kitchens or households
of 1–2 people. Slightly less energy efficient per place setting than full size.
Integrated dishwashers: fit behind a kitchen cabinet door; require an integrated-compatible
cabinet. Same compatibility considerations as integrated washing machines.

Q: What does a dishwasher's energy rating and programme duration mean?
A: The energy label shows efficiency per standard cycle (typically the Eco programme).
The Eco programme is the most efficient but often runs for 3–4 hours.
Auto or Daily programmes are shorter (1–2 hours) and suitable for normally soiled loads.
Quick wash programmes (30–60 minutes): useful for lightly soiled items but less thorough —
not a substitute for a full wash cycle on heavily soiled loads.
Most modern dishwashers above £400 in our range include a delay start function, allowing
customers to run cycles overnight on a lower energy tariff.

C4. OVENS AND COOKING APPLIANCES

Q: What is the difference between fan-assisted and fan oven?
A: Fan-assisted oven: has a heating element at the back or top/bottom with a fan to
circulate air. Good for most cooking. Most common type in our range.
Fan oven (also called a true fan or convection oven): has a heating element surrounding
the fan, ensuring even temperature throughout the oven. Better for baking and cooking
multiple trays at the same time. Cooks slightly faster and at lower temperatures than
a conventional oven.
Conventional oven (no fan): heat from top and bottom elements only. Less even temperature
distribution. Generally found only in entry-level models. Not recommended for regular baking.

Q: What is a pyrolytic oven and is it worth the extra cost?
A: A pyrolytic oven has a self-cleaning function that heats the oven to approximately 480°C,
turning food residue to ash that can be wiped away. The oven locks during this process.
The cleaning cycle takes 1.5–3 hours and consumes significant energy (approximately 2–3 kWh).
Is it worth it? For customers who cook frequently and find oven cleaning a significant
chore — yes. For light oven users — probably not, given the premium price.
NOTE: the door and surrounding cabinetry get extremely hot during the pyrolytic cycle.
Customers with young children should be advised to keep children away from the oven
during the cleaning cycle even though the door locks.
